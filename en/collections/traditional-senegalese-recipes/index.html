Traditional Senegalese Recipes

Twelve traditional Senegalese recipes: thieboudienne, yassa chicken, mafe, dibi, ceebu jen, jollof-style wolof rice, pastels, couscous, thiakry and more.

Senegalese cuisine is one of the great West African food traditions — generous, deeply spiced, built around rice, dried fish, peanut paste, and a chorus of fresh aromatics (onions, lemon, garlic, chiles). Dishes like thieboudienne and yassa are not just recipes: they're social moments, shared meals eaten from a single large platter, and family-kitchen rituals that every Senegalese cook learns young.

The twelve recipes in this collection gather the most representative Senegalese dishes: thieboudienne in both red and white versions, yassa chicken, mafe with its famous peanut sauce, grilled dibi, fried pastels, Senegalese-style couscous with lamb, and essential accompaniments (fat rice, wolof rice, thiakry for dessert). Quantities are calibrated for 4–6 people and the ingredients are ones readily available in African grocers or well-stocked international supermarkets in the UK, US, and Canada.

The non-negotiable foundations

Dried and smoked fish. Almost every Senegalese dish contains guedj (dried fish), yete (dried shellfish) or smoked fish. These are the umami pillars of the cuisine, far more important than the bouillon cube that lazy recipes substitute. If you can find guedj at an African grocer, use it — a pinch in a sauce completely transforms the aromatic depth. In its absence, a splash of Vietnamese fish sauce approximates the same effect.

Onions, properly worked. Yassa is literally an onion recipe: 6–8 onions for 4 people, thinly sliced and cooked slowly until they release their water, color, and become almost confit. This process takes 30–40 minutes and cannot be rushed without losing the character of the dish. The entire foundation of Senegalese technique requires patience with the onion.

Rice: the backbone of the one-pot meals

  • Red thieboudienne: rice cooked in a fish and tomato broth, absorbing all the color and flavor. The crispy bottom of the pot (xoon) is the most prized part of the dish.
  • White thieboudienne (ceebu jen blanc): a lighter version, rice cooked in tomato-free broth with cabbage and other vegetables.
  • Riz gras (fat rice): rice cooked with onions, tomatoes, and meat (beef or chicken) in a single pot.
  • Wolof rice: the Senegalese version of West African jollof, with tomato, onion, bell pepper, and chicken or meat.
  • Tiep bou dien (red): Wolof-dialect variant of red thieboudienne, with seasonal fish varieties.

Peanut sauces, grilled meats, and fried foods

Mafe — the peanut-paste sauce — is probably the most-exported dish of Senegalese cuisine. Its essential technique: gradually dilute the peanut paste in warm broth (never cold — it lumps), then simmer it gently with aromatics and meat for 40–50 minutes until it thickens into a creamy golden sauce. This timing also lets the peanuts' natural oils separate and rise, giving the sauce its distinctive glossy surface.

Grilled meats — dibi lamb or mutton, suya skewers — need a 2–4 hour marinade minimum (onion, lemon, mustard, chile, garlic) and quick cooking over high heat. Wood or charcoal fires add a dimension gas can't fully reproduce; if you're cooking indoors, use a very hot cast-iron grill and finish under the broiler for the last minutes.

Pastels — fried pastries filled with fish — are the quintessential street fry. The critical points are oil temperature (exactly 180 °C) and sealing the edges with water to keep them from opening during cooking.

Suggested learning order

Start with yassa chicken if you're new to Senegalese cooking — it's the most forgiving, tolerant of mistakes, and teaches the onion patience that defines everything else. Mafe is the natural next step. Reserve thieboudienne (either version) for a Sunday cook: it's a 2–3 hour project that feeds 6–8 people and justifies careful preparation. Thiakry for dessert — millet couscous with yogurt and raisins — takes 20 minutes and perfectly balances the spicy main dishes of the meal.

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between thieboudienne and ceebu jen?
They're the same dish — thieboudienne is the common French spelling, ceebu jen is the Wolof-language transcription. There are two main versions: red (with tomato, the internationally better-known version) and white (without tomato, with lots of cabbage and vegetables, more popular locally). Both use whole fish, rice cooked in the broth, and a variety of vegetables (cassava, carrot, cabbage, eggplant, sweet potato).
Where can I find guedj (dried fish) outside Africa?
African grocery stores (Peckham in London, Harlem in New York, the Goutte d'Or neighborhood in Paris) sell it in large vacuum-sealed bags. It keeps for months dry. As a substitute, use fish sauce (2 tablespoons replaces about 30 g of guedj) or a combination of dried shrimp and soy sauce for the umami character.
Can I make yassa with fish instead of chicken?
Yes — yassa fish is traditional in coastal Senegal, actually older than the chicken version. Use firm-fleshed whole fish (sea bream, grouper, thiof if available), grill it first, then simmer it 15 minutes in the caramelized onion sauce. Cooking is faster than chicken (where yassa simmers 40 minutes).
How do I get the xoon (crispy bottom) of thieboudienne right?
It's a three-phase cooking process: rice first cooks covered in the broth until absorbed, then very low heat for 10 minutes with a cloth-free lid, then medium heat for 5–8 minutes without a lid to create the golden crust at the bottom. Never stir the rice during these last 15 minutes. A slightly fatty broth (with traditional palm oil or peanut oil) helps crust formation.
Can mafe be made without commercial peanut butter?
Yes — traditional peanut paste (tigadeguena in Bambara, in Mali) is simply roasted unsweetened unsalted peanuts ground smooth. You can make your own by processing 500 g of plain roasted peanuts in a food processor for 3–5 minutes. American sweetened, salted peanut butter significantly changes the dish — look for "100% peanuts" peanut butter if you don't make your own.
Which Senegalese dishes freeze well?
Mafe, yassa, fat rice, and wolof rice freeze perfectly for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge then reheat gently. Thieboudienne is trickier — twice-cooked rice loses its texture; better to freeze the broth and fish separately and cook the rice fresh on serving day. Pastels and skewers freeze well before frying, not after.
